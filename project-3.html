Building Government's 'best kept secret'

Summary

We led a full end-to-end redesign of a high-impact government service that had long struggled with poor usability, low trust, and accessibility issues. Through in-depth user research, accessibility audits, and iterative design, we transformed the experience for both customers and case managers, making it faster, clearer, and more empowering. The new service was praised by users and case managers, and was even acknowledged in Parliament by ministers as a key step in modernising Access to Work.

Sitatution

Access to Work is sometimes referred to as Government’s ‘best kept secret’. It is a grant that can fund support to Britain’s 4.4 million people who have health conditions or disabilities that affect them at work.


I’ll use myself as an example. I am dyslexic. If I felt that I was struggling to do my job due to my dyslexia, I could apply for a funding from Access to Work for support such as dyslexia software. Despite being a fantastic service in principle, from a digital application perspective Access to Work was designed poorly and consequently leaving applicants feeling confused and offended.


Our mission was to completely refresh the service from the ground up, significantly enhancing the user experience in parallel and capturing all the complex network of data points required by policy and case managers.

Task

Our initial objective was to conduct a comprehensive discovery phase to understand why the service was underperforming and had developed a poor reputation among users. This involved identifying key pain points across the user journey and uncovering the root causes of dissatisfaction for both customers and case managers.


Using the insights gathered, it would allow us to work through our alpha and beta phases, completely redesign the service delivering a refreshed user interface, updated content, and improved user experience that met the expectations of all user groups. The redesign needed to align with the GOV.UK Design System (GDS) standards and remain within the boundaries of existing technical constraints and policy requirements.

Discovery findings

Our Discovery phase revealed a number of critical issues that were contributing to the service’s poor performance and negative user perception. Through user interviews, accessibility and design audits, and analysis of service data, we identified recurring themes that became the foundation for our design workshops and future improvements.


  We consolidated these reoccuring themes  
  • User experience: Several design patterns disrupted the natural flow of the application process, causing confusion and drop-offs. Users struggled to complete tasks due to inconsistent layouts and unclear navigation.
  • Clarity of requirements: Users were often unsure about what information was needed, why it was being requested, and how it would be used. This lack of transparency created anxiety and delays in completing applications.
  • Tone of voice: The service lacked empathy and respect in its language. Users expressed a need for a more human, supportive tone throughout the journey, especially at moments of uncertainty or vulnerability.
  • Empowerment: Many users did not feel confident or supported enough to complete the application independently. The process felt overwhelming and disempowering, particularly for those with additional needs.
  • Notifications and updates: There was no clear way for users to track the status of their application or receive timely updates. This lack of communication led to frustration and repeated contact with support teams.
  • Accessibility: Alternative formats and support options were not clearly signposted, making the service difficult to use for people with access needs. This was a significant barrier to inclusion.
  • Content quality: Some of the wording used in the service was perceived as unhelpful, or even offensive. This created a negative emotional response and further eroded trust in the service.
  • Understanding of the service: Users lacked a clear understanding of what Access to Work could provide. More upfront guidance and contextual information were needed to help users make informed decisions.


It became clear that the majority of themes uncovered during research could be grouped into two primary areas of concern:

  • Eligibility
    As with many government services, determining a user's eligibility for the grant proved to be a major pain point. In the existing journey, eligibility questions appeared too late, often after users had already invested significant time providing detailed information about their condition or disability. This led to frustration and a sense of wasted effort.
  • Lack of structure and clarity
    The previous digital journey for Access to Work was overly linear and rigid, offering little flexibility or guidance. As a result, many users were unaware of the full range of support available to them, and struggled to understand where they were in the process or what to expect next.

Action

Determining Eligibility was a major friction point in the original service. Users were often asked to provide detailed personal and medical information before being told whether they were even eligible for the grant: leading to frustration and wasted effort.


We explored several hypotheses to solve this, including:

  • An API-based eligibility checker using the user’s National Insurance number
  • A series of individual ‘yes’ or ‘no’ screener questions

While both approaches tested well in terms of clarity, they introduced new challenges, such as security concerns and increased interaction cost due to the number of screens and clicks required.


After multiple iterations, we landed on a simpler, more scalable solution: clear, upfront content paired with a checkbox component. By placing this screen early in the journey, users could quickly and confidently determine their eligibility without unnecessary effort. This approach also future-proofs the service, if eligibility criteria change, we can easily update the content or add new checkboxes without disrupting the flow.

For Lack of structure and clarity the original service was overly linear and rigid, offering little flexibility for users with varying levels of knowledge or support needs. We needed a way to accommodate both users who were unsure of what they needed and those who were ready to provide detailed information.


To solve this, we introduced the Task List pattern, a flexible, modular approach that adapts to different user journeys. Whether a user knows exactly what support they need or is just starting to explore their options, the task list provides a clear, manageable structure.

  • Don’t know what support you need? That’s okay - we’ll guide you and follow up.
  • Know exactly what you need and how much it costs? Great! Tell us, and we’ll fast-track your application.

The beauty of this pattern is its adaptability. Users can complete as much or as little as they need, focusing only on the sections relevant to them. It also gave us a scalable framework to test and iterate on each section independently.


If you're ever designing a complex service with lots of variables, I highly recommend this approach. It helped us break the journey into bite-sized, testable chunks. For example, we ran a focused sprint just on the “Your job and employer” section—allowing us to refine the content and interaction model in isolation before integrating it into the wider flow.

Result

The redesigned service was a breakthrough, transforming a previously frustrating and inaccessible experience into one that users, case managers, and even government ministers praised.

  • Customer feedback was overwhelmingly positive. Users described the new journey as clearer, more respectful, and significantly easier to navigate.
  • Case managers reported fewer support queries and a smoother intake process, thanks to clearer eligibility checks and a more structured application flow.
  • Government recognition The improvements were acknowledged in Parliament by Minister Mims Davies, who highlighted the service’s digitisation and modernisation as key steps toward making Access to Work more efficient and fit for purpose.

According to the latest Access to Work statistics:

  • Expenditure on Access to Work reached £180 million, the highest on record—indicating both increased demand and improved service delivery.
  • The number of new applicants rose by 12% year-on-year, suggesting that the redesigned service helped reach more people who previously may have been excluded or unaware of the support available.

These figures reflect not only improved usability but also a broader impact in terms of accessibility, reach, and trust in the service.
